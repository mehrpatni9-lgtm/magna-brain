Conviction is not the absence of doubt
"Conviction is not the absence of doubt. It's the decision to move anyway."
Most founders wait for certainty before committing. But certainty is a myth at the $10M → $50M stage. Every founder who made it through had the same feeling you have right now — that they might be wrong. They moved anyway. What decision have you been waiting to feel certain about?

#MagnaMondays Naval Ravikant Single Static
Your business grows faster than your identity
"Your business grows faster than your identity. That's the actual problem."
At $10M, you built the company around who you were. At $30M, the company is asking you to become someone else. Most founders resist this — quietly, without naming it. They optimize the business while their identity falls behind. The gap between who you are and who the next phase needs is the real ceiling. Not the market. Not the team. Not the strategy. That's what Magna is built for.

#WisdomWednesdays Seth Godin 5-Slide Carousel
Nike, 1984: How identity beat product
"In 1984, Nike was losing — and they didn't try to win the way everyone expected."
Carousel Slides (copy for each frame)
01
In 1984, Nike was losing.
Reebok had taken over the aerobics boom. Nike's market share was falling. The product wasn't the problem.
02
They didn't chase the trend.
Every competitor was rushing into aerobics. Nike asked a harder question instead: Who are we?
03
The answer changed everything.
Not a runner. Not a shoe buyer. An athlete. That one word reframed the entire company — product, marketing, culture, and customer.
04
"Just Do It" wasn't a tagline.
It was an identity claim. It told the market exactly who Nike was for — and who it wasn't.
05
The Magna takeaway: Identity before strategy. When you know who you are, the right customers find you — and the wrong ones stop costing you. The founders who grow past $50M don't chase markets. They claim their ground.

#FounderFridays Simon Sinek Carousel / Reel Caption
She had the answers to every question — except the one that mattered
"She'd spent 6 months preparing for the board meeting. Every financial question, answered."
$28M company. Strong margins. Clean books. Then the question came that she hadn't prepared for. Not from the board. From herself, in the silence before she walked in: Why do you still want to run this? She went quiet for a long time. That pause was the most important moment of the year. Not the meeting. Not the numbers. That moment. A company that runs itself is not the dream. Purpose is the dream. The two are not always the same — and the founders who figure out the difference early don't have to figure it out in a crisis. When did you last ask yourself why?

You don't burn out from working too hard
"You don't burn out from working too hard. You burn out from working without meaning."
There's a version of success that looks exactly right on the outside and feels completely hollow inside. A lot of founders at $20M–$40M know exactly what I'm talking about. The work didn't change. The meaning did. What still makes the work feel worth it?

#MagnaMondays Naval Ravikant Single Static
Clarity is the product
"Clarity is the product. Everything else is packaging."
You can have the right strategy, the right team, the right market — and still stall if you don't know exactly which problem you're actually solving. Most founders have excellent answers to the wrong question. The work is always the same: cut through until you see what's actually load-bearing. That's not strategy. That's thinking. And thinking is the rarest thing in business.

#WisdomWednesdays Seth Godin 5-Slide Carousel
Patagonia: The $3B brand that told you not to buy
"In 2011, Patagonia ran a full-page ad in the New York Times that said: Don't Buy This Jacket."
Carousel Slides (copy for each frame)
01
Don't Buy This Jacket.
Full page. New York Times. Patagonia's most expensive ad — and the most counterintuitive thing a consumer brand had ever done.
02
Every other brand was urging you to buy more.
Patagonia urged you to buy less. They meant it. They've never run a sale. They've never chased a trend.
03
Revenue went up 30% the year after the ad ran.
Not despite the message. Because of it. The customer who buys less, better — that's exactly who Patagonia wanted. And that customer found them.
04
In 2022, the founder gave the company away.
Patagonia's ownership transferred to a trust. The mission became the shareholder. The brand became a $3B proof of concept for conviction over compromise.
05
The Magna takeaway: The brands that endure don't optimize for every customer. They repel the wrong ones — deliberately. Clarity about who you're not for is as powerful as clarity about who you are.

#FounderFridays Simon Sinek Carousel / Reel Caption
He walked away from $4M. Two years later he sold for $22M.
"Everyone in the room thought he was crazy."
$2M in revenue. $4M acquisition offer on the table. His advisors said take it. His board pushed. His co-founder wavered. He walked away. Not because he knew it would be worth $22M two years later. He didn't. What he knew was: this isn't the number that tells the story I want to tell. That's walkaway power. Not confidence. Not bravado. Clarity about the story you're building — and the willingness to protect it even when every rational signal says otherwise. Two years later, $22M. Different story. The right one. What would you walk away from if the number felt wrong?
