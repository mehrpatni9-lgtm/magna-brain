"We will gradually stop using contractors to do work that AI can handle."
CEO Luis von Ahn said it publicly, deliberately. Headcount would only grow if teams couldn't automate more. AI would factor into hiring and performance reviews.

The backlash was immediate. Within months, he walked it back.
"I did not give enough context." Users threatened to delete the app. A year later — AI usage was quietly removed from performance evaluations entirely. "I'm not going to force you."

Slide 5 / 6 — The Magna Lens
5 / 6
[ Photo: Duolingo product / learning screen ]
The memo wasn't wrong. The sequencing was.
Duolingo had the right direction — AI will reshape language learning at scale. But declaring it before the operations, culture, and communication were ready turned a strategy into a crisis. The best bold moves are built before they're announced.

6 / 6
[ Photo: Duolingo office or bold brand image ]
The question isn't: are you going AI-first? Every serious company is.
The question is: are you building the capability before you make the claim? What are you saying you're doing — that you haven't actually built yet?
