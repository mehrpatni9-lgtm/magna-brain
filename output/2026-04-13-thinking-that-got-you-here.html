The Thinking That Got You Here

The thinking that got you to $10M is the same thinking that will quietly sabotage your path to $50M.

Most founders know something is off. Revenue is growing. The team is bigger. The problems are more complex. But the decisions are somehow harder to make, not easier.

That feeling is diagnostic.

At $10M, you were the answer to almost every question your business asked. You were fast. You were right most of the time. You built a culture that rewarded people for bringing problems to you — because you were genuinely good at solving them.

That capability is now the ceiling.

— — —

Strategy: you don't have a strategy problem

You need another framework less than you need to ask a harder question: Which problem is actually load-bearing right now?

Not the loudest one. Not the one your best manager is most frustrated about. The one that, if you removed it, would make ten other problems solve themselves.

Most founders at $10M confuse the problem that's visible with the problem that's real. They optimize brilliantly around the wrong thing for years before they realize it. Strategy, in Magna's view, isn't a framework you buy. It's the work of identifying which problem is actually the problem. Everything else is optimization around the wrong thing.

Negotiation: power doesn't come from aggression

Every significant deal you'll do from $10M to $50M will test the same thing: can you walk away?

Not as a tactic. As a genuine truth.

The founders who win in negotiation are never the most forceful. They're the most unattached. And that unattachment isn't confidence — it's preparation. It's knowing, in specific terms, exactly what you'll do if this deal doesn't close.

Your next best option isn't a backup plan. It's your power source. If you haven't built that alternative, you don't have a strategy. You have hope. And hope is a terrible negotiating position.

Culture: you set it daily, not quarterly

Culture isn't the all-hands you run. It isn't the values on the careers page.

It's the thing you let slide last Tuesday.

Every tolerance creates permission. Every permission creates expectation. By the time you notice the cultural drift, you've already been the author of it — usually by closing your eyes to the high-performer whose behavior was excused because the numbers were good.

At $50M, the behaviors you tolerated at $10M will be the exact behaviors that make talent leave and deals die. Culture is what you tolerate, not what you say. And it compounds in both directions.

Leadership: becoming who the next phase needs

The business you're building will outlast the version of you that started it.

That's not a threat. It's an invitation.

At $10M, you were celebrated for knowing everything. Every decision ran through you. That was appropriate then. At $50M, knowing everything is the bottleneck. The founders who make it through the wall aren't the ones who got smarter. They're the ones who stopped needing to be the answer.

The developmental work — becoming the version of yourself the next phase demands — is the actual variable. Everything else is noise around it.

— — —

There is only one question worth sitting with this week:

What is the one belief about how your business works that you've never seriously questioned — because questioning it would mean changing something you're proud of?

That's probably where the ceiling is.
