Principle #012: The conversation you're avoiding
MAGNA PRINCIPLE #012: "The conversation you're avoiding is the company's actual strategy problem."
Every leadership team has one meeting that never happens. The one where someone tells the CEO what everyone else already knows — but won't say. That unsaid thing isn't just awkward. It's load-bearing. The team builds workarounds around it. Processes compensate for it. Talent quietly leaves because of it. I've seen $30M companies running on collective silence for years. Everyone performing alignment. No one feeling it. The founder who can have that conversation — with clarity, without needing to win it — runs a different kind of company. One where the real problems get solved instead of quietly managed. — What's the conversation in your company that never quite gets finished?

#DigitalLectures Ray Dalio 8-Slide Carousel · Free, Ungated
Capital Allocation: The Skill No One Teaches Founders
Slide-by-slide copy
Slide 01
Title
Capital Allocation: The Skill No One Teaches Founders

Most founders are taught how to raise capital. Almost none are taught what to do with it. That gap is where most $10M–$50M growth stalls.

Slide 02
The Trap
The mistake most founders make

They treat capital allocation as a finance function. It isn't. It's a strategy function. Where you put money is where the company goes. Full stop. The CFO manages the money. The founder decides what the money means.

Slide 03
The Trap (cont.)
Bets vs. hedges

Most underfunding is not a budget problem. It's a clarity problem. When you don't know which bet matters most, you spread thin — funding five priorities at 60% instead of one at 100%. The result looks like resource constraints. It's actually a decision deficit.

Slide 04
The Shift
Stop asking "what can we afford?"

Start asking: "what does this allocation say about our strategy?" Every capital decision is a public statement to your team about what you actually value — not what you say you value. They're watching the budget, not the values poster.

Slide 05
Framework
The 3 questions to ask before any allocation

1. Does this move the needle on the one thing we've decided matters most right now?
2. Is this a bet or a hedge? (Be honest.)
3. What does approving this signal to the team about what we value?

Slide 06
Framework (cont.)
The leading indicator test

Most founders fund lagging indicators — things that report on what already happened. The founders who compound fastest fund leading indicators — the inputs that will show up in results 12–18 months from now. Talent, systems, and positioning are leading. Revenue is lagging.

Slide 07
Application
Do this audit this week

Map your last 5 capital decisions against your stated strategy. If they don't align, you don't have a budget problem. You have a thinking problem. The misalignment is the data. The question is: what decision are you actually avoiding?

Slide 08
Close
The founders who win at $50M

They aren't better at raising money. They're better at allocating it. Capital is the tool. Judgment is the multiplier. And judgment is the thing Magna is built to develop.

Principle #013: The biggest threat to a $20M company
MAGNA PRINCIPLE #013: "The biggest threat to a $20M company isn't competition. It's the founder's identity."
Every company has a ceiling that is the exact height of its founder's current self-concept. The strategies they'll attempt. The hires they'll make. The conversations they'll have — and the ones they won't. All of it traces back to who the founder believes they are. This is not motivational. It's mechanical. The company cannot outgrow the founder. And the founder can only grow through deliberate discomfort — not time. Years of experience don't change the ceiling. Years of examined experience do. I've watched founders with 15 years of hard-won scar tissue repeat the same pattern at $20M they played at $5M — because no one named it, and they never looked. — What's the one belief about yourself you've never seriously questioned — because questioning it would mean changing how you lead?

#BehindTheBuild Simon Sinek Text + Photo
Dubai Roundtable: eleven founders, one question
Dubai. 11 founders around a table. One question at the start of the session:
"What's the decision you've been sitting on for six months?" The room went quiet in a way that was different from politely quiet. These were founders running companies from $15M to $60M. Every one of them successful by any external measure. None of them had said this out loud before. Not to their boards. Not to their teams. Not to their co-founders. The conversation that followed lasted four hours. No agenda. No slides. No conclusions written on a whiteboard.
We don't run events. We build rooms where the questions that actually matter can finally be asked.
The session wasn't recorded. But the shift in the room was real.
