**TL;DR:** Shailu's negotiation framework. Three superpowers — clarity of preparation, clarity of assumptions, predicting outcomes — and one core reframe: negotiation is **not a game where someone wins and someone loses.** It is a process. You're dealing with people in a company, not the company.

## Why it matters for Magna

This is the foundation of Magna's negotiation pillar. The reframe matters more than the tactics — most founder negotiation content treats negotiation as zero-sum (anchor first, never blink, etc.). Magna's position: that frame produces worse outcomes, even by zero-sum metrics. Process beats game.

The framework is also the philosophical sibling of [[mindset-of-leverage]] — both rest on *walkaway power as preparation*, not aggression.

## The core reframe

> *"Negotiation is not a game where somebody wins and somebody loses. Look at it as a process — and walk in with what we call the superpowers of negotiation."* [[shailu-live-workshop-transcripts]]

> *"You're dealing with people in a company, not the company. What we make sure is we humanize the process — so both sides walk out gaining something."*

## The three superpowers

1. **Clarity of preparation** — do the work *before* you walk in the room.
2. **Clarity of assumptions** — name and demystify what you're assuming about the other side.
3. **Predicting outcomes** — anticipate the events that will happen, then prepare for them specifically.

> *"It's a very simple process. Thorough preparation."*

## Other sharp lines from the workshop

- **The overconfidence problem.** *"One of the foundational flaws of approaching negotiations is overconfidence. One of the foundational strengths in negotiation is preparation."*
- **Stop self-rating.** *"Stop rating yourself. Rate the quality of the transaction."* — sharp reversal worth a stand-alone post.
- **The forgotten permission.** *"You don't need to finish the deal all the time."*
- **The inevitability.** *"Negotiation is not an option. It is imposed on you whether you like it or not."*
